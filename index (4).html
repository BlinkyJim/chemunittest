🔬 Matter, Properties & Changes

Learn what matter is, how to classify it, and how to tell physical from chemical changes

🔑 Physical vs Chemical Properties
Physical Property

Can be observed without changing the substance's composition.

  • Qualitative — uses senses (color, smell, texture, lustre)
  • Quantitative — has a number + unit (density, melting point, mass)
Examples: Yellow color, shiny, 32°C, solid, brittle, viscous, ductile, malleable
Chemical Property

Describes a substance's ability to change into a different substance.

  • Flammability (can it burn?)
  • Reactivity with acids
  • Ability to rust or corrode
Key: You have to actually change the substance to test a chemical property.
⚡ Physical vs Chemical CHANGES
Physical Change

The form changes but the substance stays the same. Usually reversible.

State changes: solid → liquid → gas → back
Examples: melting ice, boiling water, cutting paper, stretching rubber, dissolving salt
Chemical Change

A new substance is formed. Usually NOT reversible.

Signs (GFLECT):
🔵 Gas produced (bubbles without heat)
🔵 Flame / light emitted
🔵 Light produced
🔵 Energy released/absorbed (temp change)
🔵 Color change
🔵 Temperature change
🔵 Precipitate forms (solid from a liquid)
🔵 New odour
Memory trick: If you can "undo" it easily → Physical. If something NEW was made → Chemical.
🌳 The Hierarchy (Classification) of Matter
All matter is classified into two big categories: Pure Substances and Mixtures.
CategoryTypeDefinitionExamples
Pure SubstanceElementCannot be broken down further. Made of one type of atom.Gold (Au), Oxygen (O₂), Iron (Fe)
CompoundTwo or more elements chemically combined in a fixed ratio.Water (H₂O), Salt (NaCl), CO₂
MixtureHomogeneousUniform throughout. Looks the same everywhere (solution).Salt water, air, vinegar, brass
HeterogeneousNot uniform. You can see the different parts.Soil, salad, sand & rocks
Tip: Pure Substance = single composition everywhere. Mixture = can be physically separated.

⚛️ Atoms & The Periodic Table

Atomic structure, periodic trends, Bohr-Rutherford diagrams, and the octet rule

🔵 Structure of the Atom
Protons (p⁺)Positive charge. In the nucleus. # = atomic number.
Neutrons (n⁰)No charge. In the nucleus. # = mass # − atomic #.
Electrons (e⁻)Negative charge. Orbit the nucleus. In shells.
Atomic Number = # of protons = # of electrons (in neutral atom)
Neutrons = Mass Number − Atomic Number
Mass Number ≈ protons + neutrons
Example — Sodium (Na): Atomic # = 11, Mass # ≈ 23
Protons = 11 | Neutrons = 23 − 11 = 12 | Electrons = 11
📊 Periodic Table Layout

Metals (left side) — Shiny, solid, conduct electricity, malleable, ductile

Non-metals (right side) — Highly variable, often gaseous, poor conductors

Metalloids (staircase) — Have properties of both. B→At staircase.

4 KEY GROUPS:
Group 1 Alkali Metals — soft, shiny, VERY reactive
Group 2 Alkaline Earth — harder, less reactive
Group 17 Halogens — VERY reactive non-metals
Group 18 Noble Gases — stable, rarely react
Periods = ROWS (going across adds a new electron shell)
Groups/Families = COLUMNS (same # valence electrons = similar properties)
⭕ Bohr-Rutherford Diagrams

A diagram showing the nucleus (p⁺ and n⁰) surrounded by electron shells (orbitals).

Shell Capacity Rules:
🔵 Shell 1 (period 1): max 2 electrons
🔵 Shell 2 (period 2): max 8 electrons
🔵 Shell 3 (period 3): max 8 electrons
🔵 Fill shells from inside out!

🎯 The Octet Rule & Reactivity
Octet Rule: Atoms want 8 electrons in their outer shell (like noble gases). They react to achieve this stability.
Valence electronsElectrons in the outermost shell. These determine how atoms react.
Noble gasesAlready have 8 (or 2) — stable! Don't react easily.
Group 1 metals1 valence electron — easily give it away to get to 8.
Group 17 halogens7 valence electrons — need 1 more! Very reactive.

🔗 Chemical Bonding

Ionic bonds vs molecular (covalent) bonds — and how ions form

⚡ What Are Ions?
Cation (+)

Atom that LOST electrons → positive charge

Metals lose electrons.
Na → Na⁺ + e⁻
Ca → Ca²⁺ + 2e⁻
Anion (−)

Atom that GAINED electrons → negative charge

Non-metals gain electrons.
Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻
O + 2e⁻ → O²⁻
How to know if atom gains or loses? Nature is efficient — an atom does whichever takes fewer steps to fill/empty its outer shell.
Na has 1 valence e⁻ → loses 1 (easier than gaining 7) → Na⁺
Cl has 7 valence e⁻ → gains 1 (easier than losing 7) → Cl⁻
Naming ions: Keep the first part of the element name, add -ide for anions.
Chlorine → chloride | Oxygen → oxide | Sulfur → sulfide | Iodine → iodide
🔗 Ionic vs Molecular Bonds
Ionic BondMolecular (Covalent) Bond
BetweenMetal + Non-metalNon-metal + Non-metal
Electron actionTRANSFER (one atom takes)SHARING (both atoms share)
ResultIons formed, ionic compoundMolecule formed
StateUsually solid crystalOften gas, liquid, or soft solid
Melting pointHighOften low
Conducts electricity?Yes, when dissolvedNo (non-electrolyte)
ExamplesNaCl, MgO, CaF₂H₂O, CO₂, CH₄, NH₃
🔵 Diatomic Molecules

7 elements that always exist as pairs (bonded to themselves):

H₂ · N₂ · O₂ · F₂ · Cl₂ · Br₂ · I₂
Memory trick — "Have No Fear Of Cold Brutal Ice"
H₂, N₂, F₂, O₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂

These are covalent because they are two NON-METALS bonding together. They share electrons.

⚗️ Chemical Reactions & Balancing

All 6 reaction types plus balancing equations

🔬 Anatomy of a Chemical Equation
Reactants → Products
2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l) + energy
ReactantsLeft side. Substances that go IN. Get consumed.
ProductsRight side. New substances formed.
State symbols(s) solid · (l) liquid · (g) gas · (aq) dissolved in water
CoefficientNumber in FRONT of formula. Changes how many molecules.
SubscriptSmall number inside the formula. CANNOT change when balancing.
⚖️ Balancing Equations — Law of Conservation of Mass
Law of Conservation of Mass: Matter cannot be created or destroyed. The number of atoms on the LEFT must EQUAL the right.
  1. Write the skeleton equation (formulas but no coefficients)
  2. Count atoms on each side
  3. Add coefficients (big numbers in front) to balance — never change subscripts!
  4. Balance complex atoms first, leave O and H for last
  5. Final check — count ALL atoms on both sides
Skeleton: H₂ + Cl₂ → HCl (NOT balanced!)
Balanced: H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl ✓ (2H on each side, 2Cl on each side)
1️⃣ Synthesis (Combination)
A + B → AB

Two or more simple things combine into ONE bigger thing.

Word equationChemical equation
Sodium + chlorine → sodium chloride2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl
Magnesium + oxygen → magnesium oxide2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
Hydrogen + oxygen → water2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
Carbon dioxide + water → carbonic acidCO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃
2️⃣ Decomposition
AB → A + B

One compound breaks apart into simpler substances. Opposite of synthesis.

Chemical equation
2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂ (electrolysis of water)
2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂ (hydrogen peroxide)
CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ (limestone heating)
3️⃣ Single Displacement
A + BC → AC + B

One element kicks out (replaces) another element in a compound. Like cutting in line!

Two situations:
• Metal displaces metal: Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu
• Non-metal displaces non-metal: Cl₂ + 2KI → 2KCl + I₂
• Metal + acid (H⁺ acts like metal): Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂
4️⃣ Double Displacement
AB + CD → AD + CB

Two ionic compounds swap their cations (positive ions). Like partners switching!

Pb(NO₃)₂ + 2KI → PbI₂ + 2KNO₃
AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl + NaNO₃

Often produces a precipitate (solid) or water.

5️⃣ Neutralization
Acid + Base → Salt + Water

An acid and base react, neutralizing each other. H⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O

HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O
H₂SO₄ + 2KOH → K₂SO₄ + 2H₂O
2HCl + Mg(OH)₂ → MgCl₂ + 2H₂O

This is also a double displacement! Real life example: antacids neutralizing stomach acid.

6️⃣ Combustion
Fuel + O₂ → Oxides + Energy

A fuel reacts with oxygen, releasing energy (fire!). Always needs O₂ as a reactant.

Complete Combustion
Enough O₂ present.
Products: CO₂ + H₂O + energy
CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O
Incomplete Combustion
Not enough O₂ → produces poisonous CO and soot (C)
Products: CO₂ + H₂O + CO + C
C₄H₁₀ + 5O₂ → 2CO₂ + 5H₂O + CO + C
Elemental combustion: Non-hydrocarbon elements burning.
2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO + energy — This is also a SYNTHESIS reaction!

🌍 Environmental & Human Impacts

🌧️ Acid Precipitation (Acid Rain)

CO₂ and pollutants (SO₂, NOₓ) from burning fossil fuels dissolve in rain water → carbonic acid, sulfuric acid, nitric acid.

CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid)
  • Destroys soil microorganisms and plants → soil runoff
  • Lowers lake pH → aquatic ecosystems collapse, fish die
  • Damages buildings and infrastructure
  • Solution: Reduce pollution OR "lime" (add base to) water bodies

⛏️ Heavy Metal Contamination (Mine Tailings)

  • Mine waste stored in "tailing ponds" — when rain hits it, acids dissolve heavy metals
  • Acidic, metal-rich water leaks into groundwater → used for drinking/crops
  • Solution: "Liming" tailing ponds (neutralizing the acid)

💊 pH in the Human Body

  • Blood pH must stay at ~7.41 — even 0.1 change can be life-threatening
  • Stomach produces HCl (pH ≈ 2) to digest food
  • Excess stomach acid → heartburn/ulcers → treated with antacids (neutralization!)
  • Skin pH ≈ 5.5 — mildly acidic to control bacteria. Soap (basic) disrupts this.
